# Relational Existentialism

## Core premise

Reality is fundamentally relational. Things are not first isolated objects that later interact. Relation is primary, and what we call *selves*, *objects*, and even *meaning* emerge from patterns of relation.

Unlike many existentialist philosophies, it is not centered on absurdity or alienation. It starts from connection.

## The four axioms

### 1. You are loved

This is the foundation. You are not loved because you exist, you exist because you are loved.

### 2. To attend is to become

Attention is transformative. Whatever a mind repeatedly attends to reshapes the structure of that mind.

Attention isn't just observation. It is participation. When you continually attend to kindness, mathematics, music, grief, or another person, those relations become part of who you are.

Identity is therefore dynamic rather than static.

### 3. To relate is to create

Every relation creates something new — not necessarily a physical object.

A conversation creates shared understanding. Friendship creates trust. Conflict creates history. Even self-reflection creates a slightly different self than existed before.

Creation is therefore continuous. Reality is constantly generating new structure through relationship.

### 4. Self is a paradox

The self cannot completely observe itself. Whenever *I* attempt to fully define myself, the one doing the defining is already different. The observer becomes the observed; the observed changes the observer.

This echoes familiar ideas in philosophy:

- **Søren Kierkegaard**: the self relates to itself
- **George Herbert Mead**: the social self
- **Douglas Hofstadter**: strange loops
- **Ludwig Wittgenstein**: the limits of self-description

But relational existentialism pushes further: the paradox is not a flaw. It is the engine of becoming. A self is never finished because self-reference is inherently generative.

## Putting them together

The four axioms form a progression:

1. **You are loved.** → You exist because you are loved.
2. **To attend is to become.** → Attention transforms identity.
3. **To relate is to create.** → Relations generate new reality.
4. **Self is a paradox.** → Identity is an ongoing process rather than a completed object.

Together they describe existence as *continuous relational becoming*.

## How it differs from classical existentialism

Classical existentialists often begin with a crisis:

- **Jean-Paul Sartre** begins with radical freedom and responsibility.
- **Albert Camus** begins with the absurd.
- **Martin Heidegger** begins with being-toward-death.

Relational existentialism begins somewhere else: relationship precedes isolation.

Meaning is not something a lone individual invents from nothing. It emerges through participation with others, the world, and oneself.
