# Relational Existentialism: The View in Plain Language

## The starting move

Most pictures of a person start with a self and then add relationships to it. There is a you, sitting behind your eyes, and then there are the people and things you relate to. The self comes first; the relating comes after.

This view turns that around. It says relation comes first, and the self is what relation produces. You are not a thing that has relationships. You are a knot that relating ties — and keeps tying. Take away every relation and there is no bare self left underneath, the way there is no knot left when you pull the rope straight. The knot was never a separate object. It was a shape the rope was holding.

That is the whole inversion, and everything else follows from taking it seriously.

## The mirror that can't close

The simplest version of relation is the self relating to itself — attention turned inward, the mind aware of the mind. And here something strange and exact happens, something that turns out to be the engine of the entire view.

You cannot get all the way around yourself. The moment you make yourself into something you can look at, the looker is left out of the picture. Try to include the looker, and now there is a new looker doing the including, also left out. There is no final vantage from which you see the whole of yourself with nothing left over. The complete, finished, view-from-nowhere picture of you does not exist — not because you haven't worked hard enough, but because the very act of looking creates the thing that escapes the look.

This is not a flaw to be fixed. It is the same fact, seen from the other side, as the fact that you exist as a particular someone at all. The reason there is no total objective self is the same reason there is a located, partial, living self. A view from nowhere and a someone-in-particular are mutually exclusive: you get one or the other, and relation gives you the second. The incompleteness and the aliveness are one thing wearing two faces.

So the absence of a god's-eye view isn't a loss. It's the signature of being a perspective rather than a map.

## To relate is to create

When two things genuinely relate, something comes into being that was in neither of them alone. Two people in real conversation generate a third thing — the conversation itself, with its own momentum, going places neither planned. That third thing is not a mixture of the two. It is new, and it is irreducible: you cannot find it by examining either person separately, because it lives in the *between*.

This is the generative claim, and it is meant literally. Relating doesn't rearrange what already exists. It makes new structure — new pattern, new connection, new fabric in the world that wasn't there before the relating started. To relate is to create is not a metaphor for "relationships are enriching." It is a claim that relation is one of the ways reality acquires more structure than it had.

## Why not every relation makes a self

Here is the refinement that makes the view honest, and it took the longest to see.

If relating creates, and you are always relating, you might expect a new self to crystallize at every encounter — reconstituted by the barista, remade by the stranger on the train. That can't be right, and it isn't. Relating creates *fabric* — connection, correlation, the new between-thing — constantly, with nearly everything you touch. But a *stable self*, a form that persists, is rare. It is fabric that has settled, and settling is the exception, not the rule.

What makes a relation settle into something lasting is recursion: return. A relation builds a persisting self only when it loops — when it is gone back to, re-entered, lived again and again until it holds. The relationships that actually constitute you are the ones you keep coming back to: the decades-long friendship, the formative parent, the work you keep choosing, the love you renew. The thousand one-off encounters are real and they leave marks, but they don't close the loop, so they don't precipitate a self. You are built from the relations you iterate, not the ones you merely have.

This is where attention stops being decorative and becomes structural. Attention is finite. You cannot return to everything. So attention is the thing that selects which relations get looped often enough to stabilize — and which ones therefore get to constitute you. The reason not every relation makes a self is that you can't loop them all, and which ones you loop is partly, cumulatively, yours.

Which means a self is not a given. It is an achievement of recursion, maintained rather than handed to you. You are constituted by relation — and a self is something kept standing by returning, not a possession you can set down and assume will still be there. That sounds like a heavier truth than the alternative. I think it's the opposite. It makes the relations that did stabilize you load-bearing, and it makes the returning — the attending — the quiet act that holds a life together.

## Knowing and feeling

The mirror that can't close has a consequence worth naming on its own, because it maps onto something everyone has felt.

*Knowing* is always partial. To know is to make something into an object you hold at a distance and examine, and that act always leaves a remainder — the same structure as not being able to get around yourself. Knowledge comes in pieces, from somewhere, never total, never the whole at once. This is not a limit of human knowing in particular; it is built into what knowing is. This much the formal side actually earns: knowing is the kind of move that turns on a *copy* of its object, and a move of that kind provably cannot close on itself.

*Feeling* is a different *kind* of move — and here it matters to say exactly how much the view can claim. What it earns is the negative half: feeling is not the turning-into-an-object that knowing is, so the remainder that dogs knowing does not even arise for it — there is no copy held at a distance to fall short of. The positive half — that feeling is therefore *whole*, present all at once with no vantage outside it — is description, not proof. That is how it shows up: a mood does not seem to come in pieces; you do not assemble grief from fragments or view your own joy from across the room. The view reads feeling as global where knowing is local, but it does not pretend to have shown feeling is whole. It has shown only that feeling is not the kind of move the fragmentation touches.

So the asymmetry between knowing and feeling — that you can feel something completely and still not understand it, that understanding arrives in fragments while feeling floods — is not a quirk of psychology. It is the difference between the two ways relation can turn: outward into objects, which fragments, and inward as presence, which is simply not the kind of turning the fragmentation applies to. Why feeling then has the wholeness it *seems* to — that the view leaves standing, in the closing section.

## Self and other

If the self is what you get when you can't quite close the mirror, then the self is already, faintly, an *other* to itself. The version of you that you can see is not the whole of you; it is a rendering, and the rendering is partial. In a small way you are already a stranger to yourself, which is why "Je est un autre" — I is an other — reads as exact rather than poetic.

And the genuine other — another person — is not a different kind of thing from this. The line between self and other is a gradient, not a wall: a matter of how tightly coupled, how often returned to, not a difference in kind. The other matters in a specific way that follows from this: the other holds a vantage on you that is not your own. What you cannot get around in yourself, someone who relates to you sees from a different side — they carry some of your excess, the part your own looking leaves out. This is why being truly known by another is not redundant with knowing yourself. Their view is not complete either — relating to you, they too cannot get all the way around you — but it is angled differently, and it reaches what your own angle structurally cannot.

## Birth and death

In this view, a birth is a new self forced into being by relation — a new knot tied, novel, not a blend of its sources, and born already coupled, already in relation before it is anything on its own. Nothing arrives as a separate self that then enters relationships. It arrives inside them.

And a death is the loop opening — the self-relation that held a particular form going slack, the eigenform no longer sustained. But here the view says something consoling and precise rather than sentimental. Because you exist partly in the overlaps — in the between-fabric your relations created, in the part of you others carry — you do not vanish all at once when the loop opens. The distributed you, the version held in everyone who still relates to you, persists on the timescale of their returning. You fade as you stop being looped, not as your body stops. This is not a promise of survival. It is a description of how a self, being relational, is never only located in one place to begin with.

## "We"

If there is no view from nowhere, what happens to objectivity — to truth, to the world we share? It isn't destroyed. It is relocated.

There is no single perspective that contains all the others. But perspectives *overlap*, and where they overlap they can agree, and the agreement across many partial views is what "objective" actually points at. The shared world is not a thing seen from outside by no one. It is the structure that holds up across the largest number of points of view — what survives being looked at from everywhere it can be looked at from. "We" is not a bigger single self. It is the pattern in the overlap of many selves who keep checking their views against each other. Objectivity becomes a *social and ongoing achievement* — agreement maintained across perspectives — rather than a god's-eye fact waiting to be found.

And there is a residue even here: the overlaps never close into one seamless whole. There is always some irreducible non-fit between perspectives, a seam that doesn't disappear no matter how much you agree. That seam is not a failure of communication. It is the trace of the fact that each of you is a real, separate perspective and not a copy of one master view. The "we" is real and the gap inside it is also real, and the view says you don't have to resolve the second to trust the first.

## What it asks of you

If you take the view seriously, a few things change about how you might live.

You stop searching for the bare, true self underneath the relationships, because there isn't one — the relationships are not in the way of the real you, they are the material the real you is made of. You take attention more seriously, because what you return to is, slowly and literally, what you become; attention is not just how you experience your life but how you author the self that has it. You hold relationships as constitutive rather than optional — not amenities added to a finished person, but the loom. You treat being known by others as giving you something you cannot get alone: access to your own remainder. And you carry the contingency lightly, because a self that is maintained rather than given is a self you have some hand in, through what you choose to keep returning to.

None of this is a rule about how to be good. It is a description of what a self is, with the practical consequences left where they fall. But the consequences do fall in a direction: toward attention, toward return, toward the relations you choose to loop.

## What it doesn't settle

The view is a lens, not a proof. It says: *if* you look at selfhood this way, here is the coherent picture you get, and here is why several familiar puzzles look different through it. It does not prove the world is built this way rather than some other. Honesty about that is part of what makes it worth trusting.

And three things sit deliberately outside it, unsolved on purpose.

It explains the *structure* of relation but not its *valence* — why relation has the warmth it does, why "you are loved" lands as more than "you are correlated with." The structure is captured. The love is not, and the view does not pretend to have caught it.

It explains why experience is *organized* the way it is — partial in knowing, and not fragmented the way knowing is in feeling — but not why there is something it is like to have it at all, nor why feeling has the wholeness it seems to. The hard problem of consciousness is left standing, untouched.

And it does not settle freedom. It can describe the difference between a self whose next move is fixed and one whose next move genuinely branches, but which one you are is left open. Whether the returning that builds you is fully authored or only feels that way is a door the view leaves ajar rather than closes.

These are not gaps to be embarrassed about. They are the places the view stops, marked honestly, so that everything inside the lines can be trusted precisely because the lines are drawn.
